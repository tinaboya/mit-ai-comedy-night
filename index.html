## Description

This is a 90-minute stand-up comedy event held on April Fools' Day, exploring one of the most pressing questions in AI research: can machines actually be funny?

The event is hosted by a visiting AI researcher from Tianjin, China — the birthplace of Chinese stand-up comedy (xiangsheng/相声) — who has spent the past several months discovering that being funny in English at MIT is somehow harder than getting a paper accepted at NeurIPS.

The evening unfolds as a live experiment. It opens with a stand-up set by the host, offering an outsider's perspective on MIT culture, AI research life, and the cultural gap between Tianjin humor and Boston humor. Then comes the experiment: several AI models are invited to tell jokes, and the audience votes in real time on whether the machines have achieved humor. Finally, the floor opens for other researchers to share their own absurd AI research moments. The host closes with a scientifically rigorous conclusion about whether AI has learned to be funny (spoiler: probably not).

This event is designed to bring together MIT's international and domestic students, researchers, and staff for a rare moment of collective laughter — especially on a day when the whole world is already primed to not take things too seriously.

## Humor Fund Justification

Humor is not a soft skill — it is a survival skill. This is especially true in AI research, where the gap between what we promise and what our models actually deliver is, frankly, hilarious. This event directly embodies the de Florez Fund's mission by demonstrating that humor matters in one of the most serious intellectual pursuits of our time. By framing comedy as a research question — can AI learn to be funny? — the event invites students to reflect on humor not just as entertainment, but as a lens through which to understand intelligence, culture, and what it means to be human.

## Humorous Impact

This event will give MIT students, researchers, and staff exactly one hour to stop pretending their work is going well and laugh about it together. By watching AI models fail spectacularly at being funny — on April Fools' Day, no less — the audience will leave with a deeper appreciation for human humor, a lighter attitude toward their own research struggles, and the shared memory of an evening.

## Summary for Publication

On April Fools' Day 2026, a visiting AI researcher from Tianjin — the birthplace of Chinese stand-up comedy — hosted a comedy event asking whether artificial intelligence can learn to be funny. The evening featured stand-up performance, a live experiment pitting human humor against AI-generated jokes, and an open-mic segment where MIT community members shared their own research absurdities. The de Florez Fund for Humor supported this event as part of its mission to bring laughter to the MIT community.
